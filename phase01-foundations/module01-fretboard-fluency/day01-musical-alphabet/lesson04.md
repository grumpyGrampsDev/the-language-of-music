# The Language of Music

> Phase 1 • Foundations
>
> Module 1 • Fretboard Fluency
>
> Day 1 • The Musical Alphabet
>
> Lesson 04 • First Note Recognition Practice

---

## Objective

Begin building the connection:

```
Mind → Note → Location → Sound
```

The goal is to begin thinking like a musician.

A guitarist sees a fret.

A musician sees a note.

---

## Lesson

Knowing the musical alphabet is the first step.

Knowing where those notes live on the instrument is the next.

Today is about building recognition.

Do not worry about speed.

Do not worry about perfection.

The goal is simply to strengthen the connection between what you know and what you play.

---

## Exercise 1 — Find the Note

Using only the Low E string, find the following notes:

```
G
A
B
C
F
F#
```

Before playing each note:

1. Say the note name aloud.
2. Find the fret.
3. Play the note.

Example:

```
G

Low E string

3rd fret
```

The note comes first.

The location comes second.

---

## Exercise 2 — Name the Note

Look at a fret number on the Low E string.

Say the note before playing it.

Examples:

```
3rd fret = ?

7th fret = ?

10th fret = ?

12th fret = ?
```

If you need to count, count.

Recognition comes through repetition.

---

## Exercise 3 — Random Note Challenge

Choose a random note from the musical alphabet.

Ask yourself:

> Where is this note on the Low E string?

Find it.

Examples:

```
Where is C?

Where is F#?

Where is A?
```

Do not measure success by speed.

Measure success by accuracy.

---

## Exercise 4 — Connect Ear and Eye

Play the following sequence:

```
E F G A B C D E
```

Listen carefully.

Notice how each note moves to the next.

The notes are different.

But they belong together.

Music is not just individual sounds.

Music is the relationship between sounds.

---

## Exercise 5 — Create a Small Melody

Using only the notes you learned today on the Low E string:

```
E F F# G G# A A# B C C# D D# E
```

Create a short melody.

It does not need to be complicated.

Three or four notes is enough.

The goal is to begin using the notes as a language.

Today I began changing how I see the guitar.

Not:

> "Where is the fret?"

But:

> "What is the note?"

The fretboard is not a collection of numbers.

It is a map of musical relationships.
