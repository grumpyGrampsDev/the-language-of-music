## Part 1: Choose Your String

The recommended string is the Low E string.

```
My chosen string: Low E

Open note: E

12th-fret note: E
```

The open string and the 12th fret have the same letter name.

The 12th-fret E is one octave higher than the open E.

## Part 3: Notes with Two Names

```
C# = D♭

D# = E♭

F# = G♭

G# = A♭

A# = B♭
```

C# and D♭ sound the same because they are the same pitch.

They have different names because a sharp describes a note as one half step higher than a letter, while a flat describes it as one half step lower than the next letter.

## Part 5: Sample Explanation

> The guitar does not contain an endless collection of different notes. Music uses seven letter names: A, B, C, D, E, F, and G. Between some of those letters are additional notes, making twelve unique notes before the pattern repeats. A sharp means one half step higher, and a flat means one half step lower. Some notes, such as C# and D♭, have two names but sound the same. At the 12th fret, the open-string note repeats one octave higher. The fretboard is a repeating map, not a list of unrelated fret numbers.

Your explanation does not need to use these exact words. It should simply include the same musical ideas.
