# The Language of Music

> Phase 1 • Foundations
>
> Module 1 Project • The Twelve-Note Tour

---

## Project Goal

You have learned that the fretboard is not a collection of numbered spaces.

It is a map of repeating notes.

For this project, you will take one complete trip through the twelve notes on your guitar and prove—to yourself—that you can name, find, hear, and explain them.

This is not a speed test.

It is a fluency test.

Your goal is to show that you are beginning to see notes instead of frets.

---

## What You Will Make

Create a short **Module 1 Project Page** in your journal, notes app, or a Markdown file.

Your completed project should include:

1. A chromatic note map of one guitar string from open to the 12th fret.
2. The correct sharp and flat names for the five notes that have two names.
3. A written explanation of the octave.
4. A recorded or live “Twelve-Note Tour” played and named aloud.
5. A short reflection on what has changed in the way you see the fretboard.

## Part 1: Choose Your String

Choose one string for your tour.

The Low E string is recommended because you have already spent time with it. If Day 04 introduced another string, you may choose that string instead.

Write its open-note name:

```
My chosen string: ____________________

Open note: ____________________
```

Then write the note at the 12th fret:

```
12th-fret note: ____________________
```

These two notes should have the same letter name.

They are an octave apart.

Check your work after you have completed it.

Ask yourself:

- Did I include all twelve unique notes before the octave repeats?
- Did I remember that there is nothing between E and F?
- Did I remember that there is nothing between B and C?
- Is the 12th-fret note the same letter name as the open string?

## Part 4: The Twelve-Note Tour

Now turn your map into sound.

Play from the open string to the 12th fret, one fret at a time.

As you play each note:

1. Say its name aloud.
2. Listen as the pitch rises.
3. Notice the two places where one natural note moves directly to the next: E–F and B–C.
4. Arrive at the octave and say its name aloud.

Your tour should sound like this in concept:

```
Open note → every note in between → same note name at the octave
```

Repeat the tour in reverse, descending from the 12th fret back to the open string.

### Challenge Round

## Part 5: Explain the Map

Write a short explanation for an imaginary beginner who thinks the guitar has “too many notes.”

Include these ideas:

- The musical alphabet has seven letter names.
- There are twelve unique notes before the pattern repeats.
- Sharps and flats describe notes one half step higher or lower.
- Some notes have two names.
- An octave is the distance from one note to the next note with the same name.
- The 12th fret repeats the open-string note one octave higher.

## Definition of Done

Your Module 1 project is complete when you can honestly say:

- [ ] I can recite the musical alphabet forward and backward.
- [ ] I know that the alphabet repeats after G.
- [ ] I know there are twelve unique notes before the pattern repeats.
- [ ] I can explain what a sharp and a flat mean.
- [ ] I know that E–F and B–C are directly beside each other.
- [ ] I recognize the five common sharp/flat note pairs.
- [ ] I can explain what an octave is.
- [ ] I can name the notes from open to the 12th fret on one string.
- [ ] I can find at least several notes on that string without using a chart.
- [ ] I am beginning to see note names where I once saw only fret numbers.
