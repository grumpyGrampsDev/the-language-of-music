# Day 03 — The Hidden Notes

> Phase I • Musical Foundations
>
> Module 1 • Fretboard Fluency

---

## Learner's Question

> **If music only uses seven letter names, why does the guitar have twelve frets before everything repeats?**

---

## Looking Back

Over the last two lessons, you've discovered two important ideas.

First, music uses only seven letter names.

```
A  B  C  D  E  F  G
```

Then you discovered that those letters repeat.

The note at the 12th fret wasn't a new note.

It was another E—one octave higher.

So now we have a puzzle.

If there are only seven note names...

Why are there twelve different notes before the octave repeats?

Where did the other five come from?

---

## A Simple Observation

Play these notes on your Low E string.

```
Open   E

1st    F

3rd    G
```

Now look carefully.

Between E and F...

there is only **one fret**.

Between F and G...

there are **two frets**.

That should feel a little strange.

If every letter were evenly spaced, those distances should be the same.

But they aren't.

Something is happening between some notes...

...and not between others.

---

## Looking More Closely

Let's ask a different question.

When you move from one fret to the very next fret, what changes?

Only one thing.

The pitch becomes just a little higher.

Every single fret raises the pitch by exactly the same amount.

That means every fret represents its own note.

So if two letter names are separated by two frets...

there must be another note hiding in between them.

---

## Filling the Gap

Let's look again.

```
E   F
```

There isn't room for another fret.

E moves directly to F.

Now compare that with this.

```
F   ?   G
```

There's an extra fret.

That fret must have a name.

Musicians call that note **F sharp**, written:

```
F#
```

Now the pattern becomes:

```
F   F#   G
```

Nothing magical happened.

We simply gave a name to the note that was already there.

---

## Discovering More Hidden Notes

Let's try another section.

```
G   ?   A
```

There's another gap.

The missing note is:

```
G#
```

Now continue.

```
A   ?   B
```

Becomes:

```
A#
```

A pattern is beginning to emerge.

Some letter names have another note between them.

Others do not.

---

## The Complete Pattern

The full musical alphabet now looks like this.

```
A  A#

B

C  C#

D  D#

E

F  F#

G  G#
```

Notice something important.

There is **no note between B and C.**

There is **no note between E and F.**

Every other neighboring letter has one note between them.

These two exceptions are so important that you'll see them again and again throughout music.

---

## Counting the Notes

Let's count every unique note before the pattern repeats.

```
1. A

2. A#

3. B

4. C

5. C#

6. D

7. D#

8. E

9. F

10. F#

11. G

12. G#
```

What comes next?

A again.

Not a new note.

The same A, one octave higher.

That's why musicians say there are **twelve notes** before the pattern repeats.

Seven natural letter names.

Five notes in between.

Twelve unique notes altogether.

---

## Try It Yourself

On your Low E string, slowly play from the open string to the 12th fret.

As you play each fret, say its note name aloud.

Don't worry about speed.

Instead, pay attention to the pattern.

Notice where there are notes between the letters...

...and where there aren't.

By the end of the exercise, see if you can answer these questions without looking back:

- Which two pairs of notes have no note between them?
- How many unique notes did you play before reaching the octave?

---

## Reflection

When you first noticed that some letters were separated by one fret and others by two, did it seem random?

Does it feel more organized now?

Can you begin to see that the guitar wasn't built with an arbitrary number of frets?

It was built around a repeating musical pattern.

---

## Language of Music Principle #3

> **Music is full of patterns. The more patterns you recognize, the less you have to memorize.**

---

## Key Takeaways

By the end of today's lesson, you should understand that:

- Every fret represents a different note.
- Some neighboring letter names have a note between them.
- Those notes are named using the sharp symbol (#).
- There are no notes between B and C or between E and F.
- Seven natural note names plus five sharp notes create twelve unique notes before the octave repeats.

---

## Looking Ahead

Today you discovered that twelve notes repeat over and over across the guitar.

But another question naturally follows.

If every fret is just one note higher than the last...

**What do musicians call the distance between two neighboring frets?**

That tiny distance turns out to be one of the most important ideas in all of music.
