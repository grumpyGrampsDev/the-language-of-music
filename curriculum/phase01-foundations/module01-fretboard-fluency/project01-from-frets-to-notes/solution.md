# Part 2 — One Complete Musical String

### Example (Low E String)

| Fret | Note |
| ---: | ---- |
| Open | E    |
|    1 | F    |
|    2 | F#   |
|    3 | G    |
|    4 | G#   |
|    5 | A    |
|    6 | A#   |
|    7 | B    |
|    8 | C    |
|    9 | C#   |
|   10 | D    |
|   11 | D#   |
|   12 | E    |

Notice:

- The musical alphabet repeats.
- E–F and B–C are naturally one half step apart.
- The 12th fret returns to the same note name one octave higher.

# Part 3 — Explaining the Ideas

### Why are there only seven letter names?

Because Western music names notes using the seven letters A through G. Instead of creating new letters, the musical alphabet repeats in higher and lower octaves.

---

### Why are there twelve unique notes?

Five additional notes exist between most neighboring letter names. Together with the seven natural notes, they create twelve unique notes before the pattern repeats.

---

### What is an octave?

An octave is the distance between one note and the next note with the same name. They sound higher or lower, but they share the same musical identity.

---

### What is a half step?

A half step is the distance between one note and the very next note. On a guitar, one fret always equals one half step.

---

### What is a whole step?

A whole step is the distance of two half steps, or two frets on the guitar.

---

# Part 4 — The Fretboard Tour

A successful tour should demonstrate that you can:

- Name each note before playing it.
- Recognize where E–F and B–C occur.
- Reach the octave naturally.
- Descend back to the open string with confidence.

The goal is not speed.

The goal is understanding.

---

# Part 5 — Finding Familiar Faces

A learner who understands Module 1 should begin making predictions before playing.

For example:

> "If this is an A on the 5th fret of the Low E string, I expect another A on the open A string."

Each correct prediction demonstrates recognition rather than memorization.

# Part 6 — Explaining the Guitar

### Sample Response

Someone new to the guitar might think there are hundreds of different notes because there are so many frets.

In reality, the same twelve notes repeat over and over across the instrument.

The fretboard isn't random. It's organized around repeating note names, consistent musical distances, and predictable patterns.

Once you understand those patterns, you stop trying to memorize every location individually and begin recognizing how the entire instrument fits together.

---

# Definition of Success

By the end of Module 1, you should be able to say:

✓ I think about notes more than fret numbers.

✓ I understand why notes repeat.

✓ I understand how musical distance is measured.

✓ I recognize patterns instead of isolated locations.

✓ I can often predict where a note will appear before I play it.

✓ I see the fretboard as one connected musical system.
